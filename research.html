Research Area
Theoretical Particle Physics

The study of theoretical particle physics concerns modelling fundamental particles and their interactions. Some practitioners care more about the modelling aspects — building effective field theories, computing cross-sections, exploring the parameter space of beyond-the-Standard-Model scenarios. Others are drawn to deeper foundational questions about the nature of the particles themselves.

My interests sit at the intersection. I am particularly drawn to supersymmetry (SUSY) phenomenology, which provides elegant solutions to the hierarchy problem while generating rich predictions for collider and astrophysical observables.

More broadly, I am interested in how the theoretical structures of high energy physics connect to cosmological observables — using the universe itself as the ultimate particle physics laboratory.

Research Area
Cosmology

Cosmology might be described as the study of where the world came from, where it stands now, and where it is bound. By "world," I mean a Cauchy surface — a snapshot of the universe at a given cosmic time. The discipline spans an enormous range of scales and methods, from primordial nucleosynthesis to the large-scale structure of the cosmic web.

The problems that motivate me most are dark energy and the fine-tuning puzzles. Why does the cosmological constant have the value it does? Can modifications to gravity account for the accelerated expansion without invoking a new fluid? These questions sit at the confluence of fundamental physics and observational cosmology.

Recently I have been working on large-scale structure (LSS) as a probe of both new physics and standard cosmological parameters, using analytic and statistical methods to extract information from galaxy surveys and related datasets.

Research Area
Classical and Quantum Gravity

Classical general relativity is, at its core, geometric analysis in disguise — the study of Lorentzian manifolds constrained by Einstein's field equations. The elegance of the geometric formulation has always attracted me, and my master's thesis on quadratic gravity with broken conformal symmetry sits squarely within this tradition.

Quantum gravity may be the last great unsolved problem in fundamental physics. The difficulties go well beyond non-renormalizability: they touch on the nature of spacetime at the Planck scale, the black hole information paradox, the emergence of classical geometry from a quantum substrate, and much more.

I approach these problems through modified gravity theories — extending the Einstein–Hilbert action in controlled ways — which provide a tractable arena for exploring both classical and semi-classical phenomena.

Research Area
Mathematical Physics

Mathematical physics occupies a contested borderland. To some it is the rigorous mathematical analysis of physical models; to others it is the use of physical intuition to discover and motivate new mathematics. I find both perspectives compelling, and prefer to inhabit their intersection rather than choose a side.

The areas that attract me most include differential geometry and its applications to field theory, the topology of fibre bundles in gauge theory, and the analytic structures that appear in exactly solvable models. I am also interested in the mathematical foundations of quantum mechanics and quantum field theory.

One of the recurring themes in my work is the interplay between symmetry and geometry — how the symmetry group of a physical system encodes deep geometric information, and how geometric data in turn constrains physical observables.
